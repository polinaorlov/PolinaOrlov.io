Winter 2026

Exploring Survivor trends

I am an avid Survivor fan. After watching the last six seasons glued to the TV every Wednesday, I became curious about a common strategic concept in the game: voting with the majority. Contestants frequently talk about being on the “right side of the vote,” sometimes even prioritizing alignment with the majority over their personal preference in a vote.

In theory, this strategy helps players avoid openly opposing others and keeps them aligned with the social leaders driving the outcome. But does it actually improve a player’s chances in the game?

To explore this question, I analyzed public Survivor data available on GitHub. I built a SQL database containing vote history and player information, used SQL and R for data cleaning and transformation, and leveraged Power BI with DAX to perform analysis and build interactive visualizations.

The resulting dashboard explores how often players vote with the majority and whether doing so is associated with stronger overall performance in the game. Check out the dashboard below!

Survivor Project
Insights

1. Majority-vote alignment appears to be positively associated with stronger game performance. However, the weak correlation signals that there are other important factors determining final position outcome for each player.

2. Winners (a.k.a Sole Survivors) are on the "right side of the vote" at least half of the time. The average Winner is in majority-vote alignment 77% of the time.

3. The relationship between majority voting and success weakens in the “New Era”, seasons 41+. The traditional “right side of the vote” strategy may be fading as a dominant path to success, likely due to increased advantages, smaller tribes, and more fluid alliance structures.

4. Individual-level vote history can reveal alliance shifts and loss of influence. Right-click on any point in the scatter plot and select Drill-through to visit the Castaway Vote Hisotry page.

Austin Bikeshare
Data Exploration

This project explores ridership patterns in the Austin Bikeshare system through an interactive Power BI dashboard built from a public BigQuery dataset. It showcases data modeling, cloud data integration, and analytical visualization techniques to uncover trends in trip duration, subscriber usage, and seasonal demand.

  • View Dashboard

Bikeshare Insights

1. Austin bikeshare demand is strongly seasonal. Average monthly trip time is generally higher in warmer months but highest in Spring and Fall months. Demand softens through late fall and winter.

2. Ridership is concentrated at a relatively small number of stations. The top 5 bike stations generates a disproportionate share of total trips in the most recent 3 years. These highlight hubs of high-demand, high-sale locations.

3. Membership-specific trends illuminate window of opportunity. Annual membership usage dip significantly in Nov-Jan; Student membership usage have a 100% growth from August to September. Targetted marketing campaigns could help boost sales in low seasons.

4. Electic is the new standard. Since early 2020, electic bikes have consistently outperformed classic in weekly trips. In 2014, there were 16 electic bike trips for each classic.
